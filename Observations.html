Module 5 – Practice: User Observation (Ramp Worker Ergonomics)

Student: Aryan Yaghobi Course: Human-Centered Design Methods I Context: Integrated Final Assessment – Part 1

Project Opportunity

How might we reduce physical strain and injury risk for airport ramp workers during baggage loading and unloading—especially in harsh weather and time-critical turns—without slowing operations?

Observation #1 — Direct Observation (Afternoon, Hot Weather)

Goal: Understand strain, coordination, and workflow under heat and time pressure. • Method: AEIOU
ActivitiesWorkers unload a B737: lifting 45–50 lb bags, twisting while stacking, scanning barcodes, moving carts to conveyors; minimal breaks.
EnvironmentsConcrete ramp, ~95°F; very noisy jet environment; 30‑min quick turn; cargo hold height ≈ 39 in causing crouched postures.
InteractionsVerbal shouts and hand signals; tight coupling between belt operator and stower; visible fatigue after ~20 minutes (pace slows, posture degrades).
ObjectsBelt loader, baggage carts, handheld scanners, gloves, knee pads. Lift-assist device present but unused—perceived as slowing the process.
UsersThree ramp agents + one lead (ages ~25–45). A newer worker mirrors peers’ motions.
Insights
  • Frequent trunk twisting and occasional overhead lifts in confined space.
  • Heat and noise elevate stress and reduce situational awareness.
  • Low adoption of assist tools due to speed culture and skepticism.
Opportunities
  • Ergonomic aids that do not slow workflow.
  • Subtle smart feedback (e.g., posture sensing, micro‑coaching).
  • Wearable support suitable for tight holds.

Observation #2 — Participant Observation (Morning, Cold & Wet)

Goal: Experience task flow to capture sensory and emotional aspects. • Method: AEIOU
ActivitiesSimulated loading with 30–35 lb crates: bending, pulling, pushing, aligning luggage; cycle: load → scan → close → secure; role rotation every 15 min.
Environments~40°F, light rain; wet, slippery tarmac; low light in hold; cold metal reduces comfort and grip; PPE used.
InteractionsHumor to manage stress; senior agent advises posture (“use knees”); supervisor monitors balance and timing; rushed yet positive team dynamic.
ObjectsBelt loader, headlamp, gloves, barcode scanner; safety cones and chalk lines marking load zones; manual belt speed control.
UsersMixed experience; one new hire shadowing a senior worker; frequent kneeling; average height 67–71 in.
Insights
  • Cold decreases dexterity and grip reliability.
  • Safety shortcuts emerge (e.g., skipping knee pads) under time pressure.
  • Cumulative stiffness develops even when fatigue isn’t obvious.
Opportunities
  • Thermal comfort and high‑grip solutions for cold/wet conditions.
  • Low-profile exoskeletal or assistive supports.
  • AI-driven rotation and rest prompts to prevent overexertion.

Cross-Observation Themes

Physical StrainBoth sessions show repeated bending/twisting under time pressure → confirms WMSD risk.
Technology AdoptionExisting lift devices observed but underused → design must feel “invisible” to workflow.
EnvironmentHeat and cold/wet conditions alter performance and safety → opportunity for adaptive gear + monitoring.
Team DynamicsInformal coaching and humor mitigate stress → designs can leverage social reinforcement.
